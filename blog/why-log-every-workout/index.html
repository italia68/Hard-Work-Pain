Why You Should Be Logging Every Workout (And How to Start)

April 2026 4 min read

There's a clear line between lifters who keep progressing year after year and lifters who look the same as they did two years ago. Most of the time, it comes down to one habit: the ones who progress are tracking their training. The ones who aren't, aren't.

You Can't Manage What You Don't Measure

Without a log, you're relying on memory. And memory is terrible at this. You might remember roughly what you lifted last week, but do you know exactly how many reps you got on your third set of Romanian deadlifts six weeks ago? No. And that means you can't intelligently progress from it.

A training log is the difference between guessing and actually knowing. Only one of those leads to consistent progress.

What to Track

  • Exercise name
  • Weight used (every set)
  • Reps completed (every set)
  • Rest time (optional but useful for intensity tracking)
  • Notes — how it felt, any form issues, energy level

How to Start

Don't overthink the format. A notes app works. A notebook works. But the best option is a dedicated tool that makes logging fast, searchable, and visible over time. That's exactly what Platemate is built for.

The first week of logging feels like extra work. By week three, it feels like the only way to train. Your past sessions become a roadmap — you can see exactly where you were, where you are, and where you're going.

The Compound Effect

A training log is a form of accountability that compounds. Every session you log adds to a record that makes skipping harder and progressing clearer. Six months of data tells you more about your body than any program you could buy.
